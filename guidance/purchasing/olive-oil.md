# Choosing supermarket olive oil

Olive oil is fresh-pressed juice that goes stale — so freshness and light protection beat the label on the front.

- **Look for a HARVEST date and buy the most recent.** A "best by" date is set by the bottler and tells you little; a printed *harvest* date is the real signal — within ~12–18 months of it is good.
- **Dark glass or tin, never clear.** Light drives it rancid; a clear bottle under bright shelf lights is a bad sign at any price.
- **"Extra virgin" alone is weak proof** — it's loosely policed, and plenty of tired oil still carries it. A reputable producer plus a harvest date beats the badge. (Seals like COOC/DOP help but rarely appear on supermarket bottles.)
- **Taste it once you're home:** fresh extra-virgin is grassy and *peppery* — a little catch at the back of the throat is a good sign (live polyphenols). Crayon, stale, or greasy-flat means it's past it.
- **Don't overpay to separate "finishing" from "cooking" oil.** One good fresh bottle does both at home; the "too nice to cook with" line is mostly marketing. *(Keeping a cheap oil for high-heat frying and a nicer one for raw is fine — just not required.)*
